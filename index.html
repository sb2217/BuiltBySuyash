Part I — Theory

The Anatomy of Type

Typography is not decoration. It is the silent architecture of meaning — the invisible grammar that determines how words land in the mind.

Specimen — Playfair Display / 400
Typography
Baseline — the invisible line on which letters rest
X-height — height of lowercase 'x' (affects readability)
Ascender — strokes rising above x-height (h, l, d, b)
Descender — strokes below baseline (p, g, y, j)
Counter — enclosed space inside letters (o, p, b)
Stem — main vertical stroke of a letterform
Terminal — end of a stroke without a serif
Kerning — space adjustment between specific letter pairs
Leading — vertical space between lines of text
Ligature — two letters joined as one glyph (fi, fl, ae)
Typeface Classification
Ag Serif

Small strokes (serifs) finish letterforms. Rooted in Roman stone carving. Optimal for long-form body text in print. Examples: Garamond, Times, Playfair.

Ag Sans-Serif

No finishing strokes. Clean, modern, geometric. Born in the 19th century. Preferred for UI and screen text. Examples: Helvetica, Futura, Gill Sans.

Ag Monospace

Every character occupies equal width. Derived from typewriter culture. Signals code, data, precision. Examples: IBM Plex Mono, Courier, Fira Code.

Ag Display

Designed for large sizes only. High contrast, decorative. Loses legibility at small sizes. Used for headlines, posters, branding. Examples: Playfair Display, Bodoni.

Ag Transitional

Bridges old-style and modern serifs. More refined stroke contrast. Highly legible at body sizes. Examples: Lora, Baskerville, Times New Roman.

Psychology of Type — Three Faces, Three Emotions

Playfair Display

Emotion: Authority · Elegance · Gravitas

High stroke contrast and sharp serifs evolved from 18th-century Transitional typefaces. The dramatic thick-to-thin transition creates visual tension that the eye interprets as confidence. Used here for all headings — the reader subconsciously attributes weight and authority to its statements.

Lora

Emotion: Warmth · Credibility · Calm

A contemporary Transitional serif with humanist proportions and moderate stroke contrast. Unlike Playfair's drama, Lora's balanced design is non-confrontational — it invites sustained reading. The slightly calligraphic quality signals that a human wrote this, not a machine. Ideal for body copy.

IBM Plex Mono

Emotion: Precision · Honesty · Technical Clarity

Monospace typefaces carry the cultural memory of typewriters, terminals, and source code. Their fixed-width rhythm is unnatural in prose but powerful in labels and metadata — it says "this information is exact." IBM Plex Mono is used exclusively for functional elements: navigation, tags, section numbers. Its contrast with the humanist serifs creates a typographic system where the reader immediately knows what is expressive and what is structural.

Color Theory — Palette Rationale

Analogous harmony — all palette colors occupy a 60° arc on the warm (yellow-orange-red-brown) spectrum. No cool tones. This creates unity and reduces visual noise. The contrast ratio between #F7F4EE and #1A1714 exceeds 14.5:1, satisfying WCAG AAA. The single accent #B5432A (burnt sienna) references manuscript ink and academic annotation — it signals importance without alarm.

Part II — Practical Application

Gestalt Principles Applied

Every design decision on this page maps to a named Gestalt law. The following index makes these applications explicit.

Law 01
Proximity

Related elements are grouped with tight internal spacing and separated from other groups by larger margins. Section labels, titles, and body text cluster together — the white space between sections communicates separation without borders.

Law 02
Similarity

Every card uses the same border, padding, and type hierarchy. Every section opens with the same monospace label → serif title → italic lead pattern. Readers learn the system after two repetitions and can navigate unconsciously.

Law 03
Continuity

The left-edge border rule in the hero, the vertical reading flow, and the step-arrow connectors in the process section all guide the eye in a single direction. No element interrupts the reading path.

Law 04
Figure–Ground

Warm paper (#F7F4EE) versus near-black ink (#1A1714) creates a 14.5:1 contrast ratio. Text is always the figure; background is always ground. The single dark-band section (manifesto) is a deliberate inversion — making it a visual landmark.

Law 05
Closure

Flip cards (GitHub projects) present the front face as an incomplete unit — the reader knows more exists behind. The act of flipping completes the informational closure. Card borders define closed perimeters that group content internally.

Law 06
Common Fate

All reveal animations move upward at the same speed and easing. All hover states apply the same offset-shadow. This shared motion behavior signals that cards belong to the same system — they share a "fate" as interactive objects.
